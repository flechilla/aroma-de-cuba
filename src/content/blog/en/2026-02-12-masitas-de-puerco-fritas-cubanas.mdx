## The art of frying pork the Cuban way

Some dishes need no introduction at a Cuban table. **Masitas de puerco fritas** are one of them: golden chunks of pork that crackle when you bite in, bathed in the unmistakable aroma of [mojo criollo](https://icuban.com/food/mojo_criollo.html), served with raw onion rings and a generous squeeze of lime.

This isn't fancy restaurant food. It's **farm food**, **country party food**, **Sunday family food** for when someone decides today we eat well. And in Cuba, eating well almost always means pork.

## From Eastern farms to the national plate

Masitas de puerco have deep roots in **Cuban peasant tradition**, especially in the eastern provinces where pig farming was a fundamental part of rural life. Unlike [lech√≥n asado](/en/blog/2026-02-11-lechon-asado-cubano/) ‚Äî which required a whole pig and hours of preparation ‚Äî masitas were the practical version: pork chunks any family could prepare on any given day.

The technique is simple but precise: **marinate, boil, and fry**. That three-step process is what distinguishes Cuban masitas from similar preparations across the Caribbean. The mojo marinade ‚Äî built on sour orange, crushed garlic, and cumin ‚Äî doesn't just season the meat, it **tenderizes** it through the citrus acid.

In Havana, masitas became stars of the **fondas and cafeter√≠as** that fed workers from the 1940s onward. Served on brown paper with a piece of Cuban bread, they were the perfect lunch: hearty, flavorful, and cheap.

## The authentic recipe: no shortcuts

### Ingredients (serves 6-8)

- **2.5 lbs pork shoulder**, cut into 2-inch chunks
- **1 cup sour orange juice** (or 50/50 mix of orange and lime juice)
- **10 garlic cloves**, crushed in a mortar
- **1 teaspoon ground cumin**
- **1 teaspoon dried oregano**
- **Salt and black pepper** to taste
- **2 cups water**
- **¬º cup lard** (or vegetable oil)
- **1 large onion**, sliced into rings
- **2 limes**, cut into wedges

### The mojo marinade

In a mortar ‚Äî or a blender if you don't have one ‚Äî crush the garlic with salt into a paste. Mix with sour orange juice, cumin, oregano, and pepper. This is your **raw mojo**, the soul of the masitas.

### Step-by-step preparation

1. **Marinate**: Place the pork chunks in a deep container, cover with the mojo, and refrigerate for **at least 4 hours**, ideally overnight. Turn the meat at least once.

2. **Boil**: Transfer the pork with all its marinade to a large pot. Add 2 cups of water. Cook over medium-low heat for **45 minutes** until the meat is tender and the liquid has reduced almost completely.

3. **Fry**: Here's where the magic happens. In a heavy skillet (a [Cuban caldero](https://asassyspoon.com/masitas-de-puerco-cuban-pork-chunks/) is ideal), heat the lard over medium-high heat. Fry the pork chunks without crowding, turning until **golden and crispy** on all sides, about 8-10 minutes per batch.

4. **Serve**: Arrange the masitas on a platter, top with raw onion rings, and squeeze lime all over. Some cooks prepare an additional **hot mojo** ‚Äî garlic fried in olive oil with sour orange ‚Äî to drench the masitas just before serving.

## Secrets not written in the recipe

**The lard matters.** Masitas fried in lard have an unmatched flavor. Vegetable oil will give you good results, but not *Cuban* results.

**Don't rush the boil.** The pork needs to cook slowly so the collagen breaks down and the meat turns tender before frying. If you fry it raw, it'll be tough outside and flavorless inside.

**The size of the cut.** Not too big, not too small: 2-inch chunks allow the center to stay juicy while the surface gets golden. It's the same philosophy behind [Mexican carnitas](https://www.food.com/recipe/masas-de-puerco-cuban-fried-pork-192714), close cousins of masitas.

**The onion stays raw.** You don't fry it, you don't saut√© it. Raw onion in thick rings is the fresh, sharp counterpoint that balances the richness of fried pork.

## Masitas in the diaspora

In Miami, masitas de puerco are a mandatory presence at the ventanitas on Calle Ocho and in Cuban restaurants across Hialeah. [Versailles](https://www.versaillesrestaurant.com/), the gastronomic cathedral of Cuban exile, serves them with their house mojo ‚Äî they never leave the menu.

In Tampa, where the Cuban community has roots stretching back to the 19th century, masitas share the table with the [Cuban sandwich](/en/blog/2026-02-11-sandwich-cubano/) at lunch counters feeding Ybor City workers.

What's remarkable is that the recipe has barely changed. Unlike other dishes that adapt and fuse, masitas de puerco are prepared today in Hialeah exactly as they were in Camag√ºey 80 years ago. Perhaps because perfection doesn't need innovation.
