If you've ever walked into a Cuban home at lunchtime, the first thing that hits you isn't the sight of the steaming pot â€” it's the **aroma**. That unmistakable perfume of onion browning with garlic and pepper in hot oil has a name: **sofrito**.

## What is Cuban sofrito?

Sofrito is the **aromatic base** upon which nearly all Cuban creole cooking is built. It's not a dish in itself but rather the first step of virtually any recipe involving a caldero (cooking pot): [black beans](/en/blog/2026-02-11-moros-y-cristianos/), [ropa vieja](/en/blog/2026-02-10-ropa-vieja-plato-nacional-cuba-receta-historia/), [picadillo](/en/blog/2026-02-12-picadillo-cubano-habanera/), [arroz con pollo](/en/blog/2026-02-12-arroz-con-pollo-cubano/), and so much more.

Unlike other aromatic bases around the world â€” the French *mirepoix* (onion, carrot, celery) or the Italian *battuto* â€” Cuban sofrito has its own character defined by three inseparable ingredients: **onion, garlic, and pepper**.

## The sacred ingredients

### The Cuban trinity

1. **White or yellow onion** â€” The sweet base. Diced finely and cooked until translucent.
2. **Fresh garlic** â€” Generous, always fresh, never powdered. Cubans don't skimp on garlic.
3. **AjÃ­ (cubanelle pepper)** â€” The Cuban green pepper, milder than common green bell pepper, brings an herbaceous flavor without heat.

### The supporting cast

- **Tomato** â€” Fresh or as paste, adding acidity and color. Depending on the dish, it may be a star or absent entirely.
- **Olive oil or vegetable oil** â€” The medium where everything transforms. Traditionally, lard was used.
- **Cumin** â€” A pinch that marks the difference between a good sofrito and a memorable one.
- **Dried oregano** â€” Rubbed between your palms before adding to release its essential oils.
- **Dry white wine (vino seco)** â€” The finishing touch that [deglazes](https://www.thespruceeats.com/what-is-deglazing-a-pan-995570) the pan and adds depth.
- **Bay leaf** â€” Added to longer-cooking stews, infusing its aroma throughout.

## How to make the perfect sofrito

### Step by step

1. **Heat the oil** over medium heat in a wide skillet or caldero. The oil should be hot but not smoking.
2. **Add the onion** diced finely. Cook 3-4 minutes until translucent and beginning to brown.
3. **Add the pepper** finely chopped. Cook 2 more minutes.
4. **Add the garlic** crushed or very finely minced. This is crucial: **1 minute maximum**. Burnt garlic will make the whole dish bitter.
5. **Add tomato** (if the recipe calls for it), cumin, and oregano. Cook 2-3 minutes.
6. **Deglaze with dry wine** if desired, scraping the bottom to integrate the caramelized flavors.

### Abuela's secrets

- **Don't rush the process.** Sofrito needs time and medium heat. Cranking up the flame doesn't save time â€” it just burns the ingredients.
- **Garlic always goes in last** among the trinity. It's the most delicate and burns in seconds.
- **Chop everything before you start.** Once the oil is hot, there's no time to dice.
- **Listen to the pan.** A constant sizzle indicates the right temperature. If it goes quiet, it needs more heat. If it splatters aggressively, turn it down.

## History of sofrito in Cuba

The word *sofrito* comes from the Catalan *sofregit* ("to lightly fry"), arriving in Cuba with Spanish colonizers in the 16th century. But what we know today as Cuban sofrito is the result of **centuries of culinary blending**:

## Batch sofrito: your secret weapon

To always have sofrito ready:

1. Prepare a large quantity (5-6 onions, whole head of garlic, 4-5 peppers)
2. Cook to the perfect point
3. Cool completely
4. Distribute into ice cube trays
5. Freeze and transfer to airtight bags
6. **Lasts up to 3 months** in the freezer

Each cube equals approximately 2 tablespoons â€” perfect for a dish serving 4.
